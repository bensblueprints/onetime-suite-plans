## PART 1 — THE PROBLEM: you used to buy software (2:00–6:30)

[B-ROLL: 2000s-era software boxes on a shelf — Photoshop CS, Office 2003]

Here's something younger viewers might not even remember: software used to be a
*product*. You walked into a store, you paid once, you owned it. Photoshop was six
hundred dollars — a lot of money! — but it was yours. Forever. People ran the same
copy for ten years.

Then, around 2012, the industry discovered something wonderful — wonderful for
*them*: the subscription. And look, I'm not a conspiracy guy. Subscriptions make
honest sense when a company does ongoing work for you — hosting your files,
delivering your email, running servers you'd rather not run. Some subscriptions are
fair.

But somewhere along the way, everything became a subscription — including tools where
**your own computer does all the work.**

[B-ROLL: remove.bg pricing page]

Think about what remove-dot-bg actually does. You upload a photo, an AI model removes
the background, you download it. That model — the same class of model — runs happily
on the laptop you already own. But instead, you pay nine dollars a month for forty
credits, forever, and every photo you process gets uploaded to their servers first.

You are paying rent — on your own hardware.

[B-ROLL: split-screen: PDF tools, screen recorders, transcription apps, each with
"$X/mo" tag]

Merging a PDF. Recording your screen. Transcribing a meeting. Compressing images.
Making a meme, an org chart, a color palette. These are jobs your machine does
locally, in seconds — and the industry has convinced everyone that each of them is
worth ten to forty dollars a month, per person, forever.

And the subscription model has three other costs nobody puts on the pricing page:

**One — the prices only go up.** Linktree Pro went from about nine dollars to fifteen
in one jump. When you're locked in, when your bio link or your customer history lives
on their servers, what are you going to do — leave? They know you won't.

**Two — your data is a hostage.** Your analytics live in their dashboard. Your
subscriber list lives in their CRM. Your recordings live in their cloud. Every month
you pay isn't just rent for the tool — it's ransom for your own history.

**Three — they can simply disappear.** Tools get acquired, "sunset," repriced,
feature-gated. If you've been on the internet more than five years you've lost at
least one tool you loved this way. When you don't own the software, every one of your
workflows is built on someone else's business decisions.

[BEAT]

So that's the problem, plainly: **you're paying rent, forever, at rising prices, for
work your own hardware does, while someone else holds your data.**

Everybody feels this. Almost nobody does anything about it, because the alternative —
finding or building a replacement for every single tool — sounds like a full-time
job.

So I made it my full-time job.

If you're a developer, or you have one on staff, and you've got the evenings to spare

## PART 4 — THE CATCH, STATED PLAINLY (16:30–20:00)

There are two catches. Neither is fake. Here they are.

**Catch one: the price is going up — on a schedule, not as a stunt.**

[B-ROLL: Door Tracker page showing "$49 intro → $299 at launch"]

This catalog is priced at what I'd call *builder prices*. Door Tracker is forty-nine
dollars right now against competitors charging over a hundred dollars per rep per
month — and its own page tells you it goes to two ninety-nine at full launch. Same
story across the suite: today's individual prices are introductory, and the $997
bundle is priced against the *current* fifty-eight apps — not the hundred-plus it
becomes. Every five apps that ship make this bundle more valuable and give me
another reason to reprice it. The nine-ninety-seven number cannot survive the
catalog it's attached to. When it moves, it moves up.

**Catch two: this offer is capped at one hundred people.**

Not "a hundred" as a marketing flourish — a hundred because of what's included.
Bundle owners aren't just customers; at this stage of the challenge they're
effectively founding members. They get every future app free, forever, and their
feature requests are what steer the build sheet — I'm building forty-six more apps,
and the people who own everything are the people I build for first. I can make that
promise to a hundred people and keep it. I can't make it to ten thousand. So the
first hundred through this video get the $997 price and the everything-forever deal
— and after that, the offer changes shape.

[BEAT]

If you're watching this after the hundred are gone — the free door never closes, the
per-app door never closes, and I'd rather you take either of those than feel played.
But if the buy button below this video still shows nine ninety-seven... you're inside
the window.

## PART 5 — WHO THIS IS FOR, WHO IT ISN'T (20:00–22:00)

Let me save some of you the money. **Don't** buy the bundle if:

- You genuinely only need one or two tools. Buy those two apps for under sixty bucks
  total, or build them free from GitHub. That's the right call and I just told you so
  on camera.
- You need deep enterprise features today — SSO across everything, SOC 2 paperwork,
  a vendor to sue. This is not that, and pretending otherwise would burn us both.
- You can't run a five-dollar VPS and don't want to learn. The desktop apps still
  work for you, but half the catalog's value is self-hosted web apps.

**Do** buy the bundle if:

- You're a founder, freelancer, agency or small team whose statement looked anything
  like the one at the top of this video.
- You believe your data should live on hardware you control — as a principle, not
  just a preference.
- You've ever lost a tool you loved to an acquisition, a price hike or a shutdown,
  and you're done building your business on rented ground.
